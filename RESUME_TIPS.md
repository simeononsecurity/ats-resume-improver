## The ATS Reality Check

Most companies screen resumes through an Applicant Tracking System before a human ever reads them. Here's what that means in practice:

- **ATS scores your resume against the job description.** It looks for matching keywords, titles, and phrases. A well-written resume full of synonyms can score low because it used different words.
- **Fancy formatting breaks ATS parsing.** Tables, columns, text boxes, headers/footers, and graphics often get scrambled or dropped entirely when the system tries to extract your text.
- **Your resume may never fail — it just never ranks high enough to be seen.** You don't get a rejection for bad formatting; you just disappear.
- **The ATS score isn't the only thing that matters.** Once a human sees it, the resume has to look clean and tell a clear story in under 10 seconds.

Design for both. ATS first, human second.

## Formatting Rules That Matter

### Do This

- **Use a single-column layout.** Two-column and sidebar layouts cause ATS parsers to read the columns in the wrong order or skip content.
- **Stick to standard section headers.** "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," "Projects," "Certifications" — not creative names like "Where I've Been" or "My Toolkit."
- **Use clean fonts.** Calibri, Garamond, Georgia, Arial, or Times New Roman. Nothing that requires a font download.
- **Font size 10–12pt for body text, 14–16pt for your name, 11–13pt for section headers.**
- **Keep margins between 0.5" and 1"** — tight enough to fit content, loose enough to breathe.
- **Use real bullet points** (•), not dashes, hyphens, or custom symbols. Some ATS systems misparse non-standard characters.
- **Bold job titles, company names, and dates** to make them scannable.
- **Consistent date formatting.** Pick one style: `Jan 2022 – Mar 2024` or `01/2022 – 03/2024` and stick to it everywhere.

### Avoid This

- Tables, text boxes, and columns
- Headers and footers (ATS often ignores or garbles them)
- Graphics, logos, and profile photos
- Horizontal lines used as decorative elements (a single rule under your name is fine)
- Color beyond black and one accent — it doesn't help ATS and rarely helps humans
- Fancy templates from Canva or design tools — they look great as PDFs but parse terribly

## Writing Your Summary

The summary is the first thing read and the easiest place to lose someone. Write it last, after you know exactly what the resume is selling.

### What a Good Summary Does

- Tells the reader your level, your field, and your primary value in 2–4 sentences
- Mirrors the language of the role you're targeting
- Doesn't waste space on things any applicant could say ("hard worker," "team player," "passionate")

### Formula That Works

> [Title/Level] with [X years] in [Domain]. Specialized in [specific strength]. Track record of [key achievement or impact]. Seeking [what you want, optional].

**Example:**
> Software engineer with 6 years in backend infrastructure. Specialized in distributed systems and high-throughput data pipelines. Built and scaled services handling 50M+ daily events at two Series B startups.

### What to Skip

- "Results-driven professional with a passion for excellence" — this says nothing
- Objective statements at senior level (fine for entry-level or career changers)
- Anything that's just a restatement of your title and industry

> **Tip:** If your summary could be copy-pasted onto someone else's resume without changing a word, rewrite it.

## Bullet Points That Land Interviews

Bullet points are the core of your resume. Most people write job descriptions. What you want is **accomplishments written as evidence**.

### The Formula: Action + What You Did + Result

> **[Strong verb]** + **[what you actually did]** + **[measurable result or scope]**

**Weak:** `Responsible for managing cloud infrastructure`
**Strong:** `Reduced AWS infrastructure costs 34% by migrating legacy EC2 workloads to containerized services on ECS`

### Strong Opening Verbs

Built, Designed, Led, Reduced, Increased, Shipped, Automated, Migrated, Refactored, Launched, Negotiated, Trained, Audited, Consolidated, Scaled, Deployed, Architected, Streamlined, Resolved, Owned

Avoid: *Responsible for, Assisted with, Helped, Worked on, Participated in*

### Numbers Matter — Even Rough Ones

If you don't have exact numbers, use ranges or context:
- "~20% reduction" is fine
- "team of 8" adds context
- "3M+ users," "50+ client accounts," "Q3 2023 launch" all signal real scope

If you truly have no metrics, focus on **scope and ownership**: "Sole engineer on the authentication service serving all enterprise clients."

### Bullet Length

- Aim for 1–2 lines per bullet, 3 max
- If you need 3 lines, you probably have two bullets trying to be one
- 3–5 bullets per role is the sweet spot for most positions

### Don't List Duties

ATS and recruiters already know what a software engineer does. What they don't know is **what you specifically did and how well**. Every bullet should answer: *So what?*

## Keywords & How to Use Them Right

### How to Find the Right Keywords

1. Pull the job description text
2. Look for repeated nouns and technical terms — those are what the ATS is scoring
3. Compare them to your resume word-for-word
4. Add missing terms where they're genuinely true for your background

### Where to Put Keywords

- **Summary** — 1–3 core terms woven in naturally
- **Skills section** — direct keyword match, comma-separated or in categories
- **Bullet points** — worked into sentences describing real work, not just dropped in
- **Job titles** — if your title differs from the industry standard, you can add a clarifier: `Senior Engineer (Software Development Engineer equivalent)`

### What Not to Do

- Don't keyword-stuff. "Managed Python Python Python Django REST APIs" doesn't work and looks terrible to humans.
- Don't claim skills you don't have. You'll get asked about them in the interview.
- Don't use acronyms without spelling them out at least once. ATS may look for "Kubernetes" not "K8s" — use both.

> **Tip:** Paste both the job description and your resume into the ATS analyzer in this tool. It will show you exactly which keywords you're missing and which ones you've matched.

## The Skills Section

### When to Include It

Almost always. It's one of the fastest ways to add keyword density and it's the first place many technical recruiters scan.

### How to Structure It

Group by category rather than listing everything in one block:

```
Languages:     Python, Go, TypeScript, SQL
Frameworks:    Django, FastAPI, React, Node.js
Infrastructure: AWS, Terraform, Kubernetes, Docker
Tools:         GitHub Actions, Datadog, PagerDuty
```

### What to Leave Out

- Skills you'd be embarrassed to answer a question about
- Tools you used once four years ago and barely remember
- Soft skills ("communication," "teamwork") — these belong in bullet points as demonstrated behaviors, not as line items

### Level Indicators

Most people skip these but they can add clarity:
- "Proficient in Python (5 yrs), working knowledge of Go"
- Or just list by category and let the bullet points establish depth

## Experience Section

### Order

Most recent first. Always.

### What to Include per Role

- Company name, your title, location (city/state or "Remote"), and dates
- 3–5 bullets focused on accomplishments
- Tech stack or tools used (can be in bullets or a brief line below the title)

### Gaps in Employment

You don't need to explain every month. If there's a gap of 6+ months, a brief note is better than silence:
- `Career break — family care, 2023`
- `Independent consulting / freelance, 2022–2023`
- `Full-time bootcamp / credential program, 2021`

Trying to hide gaps by using only years instead of months (2019–2021 vs. Jan 2019–Mar 2021) is common but most recruiters will ask. Just be honest.

### Contract, Freelance, and Consulting Work

List it. Group shorter contracts under a single "Freelance / Contract" entry if there are many, or list clients individually if the names add credibility.

## Projects Section

Projects are often the most underused section, especially for early-career candidates and career changers.

### When Projects Matter Most

- You're applying to technical roles and want to show hands-on skills
- You're early-career with limited professional experience
- You're switching industries and want to show you've applied skills in the new domain
- The job requires a specific technology you haven't used professionally but have built something with

### What to Include per Project

- Project name (link to GitHub or live demo if possible)
- 1–2 sentences: what it does and why it exists
- Technologies used
- Scale or outcome if applicable (users, stars, deployment status)

**Example:**
> **OpenBudget** — [github.com/user/openbudget](https://github.com)
> Personal finance tracker built with React and a Go backend, deployed on Fly.io. Used by ~200 people since launch. Integrated Plaid API for bank syncing and automated monthly category reports.
> *Stack: React, Go, PostgreSQL, Plaid API, Fly.io*

### What to Skip

- Tutorial projects copied from a course ("Todo App," "Weather App")
- Unfinished projects with no code in the repo
- Projects so old the tech stack is outdated and you can't speak to them

## Education Section

### Where to Put It

- **Recent grad (0–3 years):** Above experience, right below summary
- **Experienced professional:** Below experience — your work history is more important

### What to Include

- Degree, major, university, graduation year
- GPA if 3.5+ and within the last 5 years
- Relevant coursework only if directly relevant to the job and you lack experience in that area
- Honors, dean's list, relevant clubs, or leadership roles if they add something substantive

### What to Skip

- High school once you have a college degree
- GPA below 3.5
- Coursework that's just the standard curriculum
- The full address of the university

## Cover Letters

### Do You Actually Need One?

If the application has a cover letter field that's optional, write one anyway — it signals effort. If the field isn't there, skip it.

### What a Cover Letter Should Do

It's not a summary of your resume. The hiring manager can read. A cover letter should:

1. Show you understand the company and the specific role
2. Connect one or two specific experiences from your background to what they need
3. Give a sense of your voice — something a resume can't do

### Structure That Works

- **Opening:** Why this company, specifically. Not "I am excited to apply for the Software Engineer role at Acme Corp." Something real.
- **Middle:** 1–2 paragraphs connecting your background to the role's actual challenges. Reference a bullet or project from your resume and expand on the "why" behind it.
- **Close:** Clear, short. What you're hoping for. A direct ask for a conversation.

### Tone

Write like a person. Contractions are fine. Short sentences are fine. You're not filing a legal brief.

**Avoid:**
- "I am writing to express my sincere interest in..."
- "I would be a valuable asset to your team"
- "leverage," "synergy," "passionate about," "excited to contribute"
- Starting multiple sentences in a row with "I"

**Aim for:** The way you'd explain your background to a smart friend of a friend who asked why you wanted this job.

## Tailoring vs. One-Size-Fits-All

### The Hard Truth

A generic resume will always underperform a tailored one. ATS systems compare your resume to the specific job posting — a resume tuned for a different role, even in the same field, will score lower.

### What Tailoring Actually Means

You don't rewrite the whole thing. You:

1. **Swap out or add keywords** from the job description into your summary and bullets where accurate
2. **Reorder your bullets** so the most relevant ones are first in each role
3. **Adjust your summary** to reflect what this specific company needs
4. **Add or remove the projects section** depending on relevance
5. **Check your job title** — if it's non-standard, add a parenthetical that matches their language

### What It Doesn't Mean

- Making up experience you don't have
- Completely rewriting bullets for every job
- Using a different resume for every single application (maybe 3–4 base versions for different role types, then light tailoring per application)

> **Tip:** Use this tool to run the ATS keyword match. Anything under 70% match is worth a quick keyword pass before submitting.

## Common Mistakes to Cut Right Now

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Objective statement for experienced candidates | Replace with a targeted summary |
| "References available upon request" | Delete it entirely — assumed |
| Responsible for / Assisted with / Helped | Use action verbs with outcomes |
| Listing every job since high school | Only go back 10–15 years for experienced candidates |
| Personal info (photo, age, marital status) | Remove — not appropriate in US hiring and can cause bias |
| Unexplained acronyms | Spell out at least once |
| Skills listed with no context or depth | Group by category, let bullets prove depth |
| Using "we" throughout | Say "I" or own the action in the verb: "Led the team to..." |
| One resume for every job | At minimum, tune the summary and keywords per industry |
| Saving as .doc or .docx only and using weird fonts | Submit .pdf for human review, plain formatting throughout |

## Length & Page Count

### One Page

- New grad or under 5 years of experience
- When the company or role explicitly asks for it
- Roles in fields where brevity is cultural (some startups, design roles)

### Two Pages

- 5–15 years of experience — two pages is standard and expected
- Complex technical backgrounds with many meaningful projects or roles
- Senior IC, staff, or management roles with a longer track record

### Three+ Pages

Almost never needed unless you're an academic (CV territory), a C-level with a 20+ year career, or in a niche field where full publication/patent lists are required.

**The rule:** every line should earn its place. If you're struggling to fill page two, that's fine — don't pad it. If you can't get under three pages, start cutting the oldest and least relevant experience.

## File Format & Submission

### When to Submit PDF

Almost always. PDF preserves your formatting exactly as you designed it.

### When to Submit .docx

When the job posting or ATS specifically asks for Word format. Some older ATS systems actually parse .docx better than PDF. If in doubt and both are accepted, submit both or ask.

### Naming Your File

`FirstLast_Resume.pdf` or `FirstLast_Resume_CompanyName.pdf`

Never: `resume_final_v3_FINAL_USE_THIS.pdf`

### Email Submissions

- Subject line: `Application for [Job Title] — [Your Name]`
- Keep the email body brief. 3–4 sentences max. The resume is attached; don't re-summarize it.
- If you were referred, mention the person's name in the first sentence.

## Before You Hit Send

Run through this checklist:

- [ ] Contact info is current and correct (email, phone, LinkedIn, city/state)
- [ ] No typos — spellcheck + read it out loud
- [ ] Dates are consistent and accurate
- [ ] Every bullet starts with a strong action verb
- [ ] At least 60–70% keyword match with the job description (run the ATS analyzer)
- [ ] No tables, text boxes, or columns
- [ ] File is named properly
- [ ] PDF opens correctly on another device or in a browser
- [ ] Someone else has read it (a second set of eyes catches what you miss)
- [ ] LinkedIn profile matches your resume's dates and titles
- [ ] You can speak to every item on this resume confidently

## Quick Reference: Word Choices

### Replace These

| Don't Use | Use Instead |
|---|---|
| Responsible for | Led, Owned, Built, Managed |
| Assisted with | Contributed to, Co-led, Supported (with specifics) |
| Worked on | Developed, Shipped, Refactored |
| Helped improve | Improved (own the verb) |
| Was part of a team that | Led / Collaborated on (be specific about your role) |
| Passionate about | (just show it through your work) |
| Leverage | Use |
| Utilize | Use |
| In order to | To |
| Due to the fact that | Because |
| At this point in time | Now |
